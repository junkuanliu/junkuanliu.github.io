Research

My research focuses on machine learning applications in biomedical informatics and enhancing the capabilities of large language models. The following are my primary research areas:

Antibody Sequence Modeling

Working with the Biomedical & Clinical Informatics Lab (BCIL) at the University of Michigan, I focus on developing and optimizing variational autoencoder (VAE) architectures for antibody sequence modeling. This research aims to improve our understanding of antibody functionality and enable more effective antibody design for therapeutic applications.

LLM Reasoning and Verifiability

I investigate methods to enhance the reasoning capabilities and verifiability of large language models, particularly for complex reasoning tasks. My work on the Tiered Reasoning for Intuitive Physics (TRIP) benchmark has demonstrated significant improvements in accuracy and verifiability through advanced prompting strategies and cross-dataset transfer learning.

  • Tackled spurious correlations and weak lower-level reasoning in LLMs for the Tiered Reasoning for Intuitive Physics (TRIP) benchmark, driving notable accuracy improvements.
  • Prevented domain overfitting by applying cross-dataset transfer learning (PIQA → Conversational Entailment), boosting out-of-distribution generalization by 2%.
  • Strengthened LLM reliability with advanced prompting strategies (conflict-type balancing, object substitution, role-play prompts), increasing accuracy by 31.4% and verifiability by 57.4% on TRIP.
  • Avoided HPC resource bottlenecks on Great Lakes by accurately estimating computational requirements and optimizing resource allocation, ensuring stable training efficiency.
